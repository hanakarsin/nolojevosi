Martha Koome Discloses She Teaches Sunday School When not in Corridors of Justice

July 11, 2024
Post
  • Chief Justice (CJ) Martha Koome has disclosed how she spends time outside her busy office
  • When not in her judicial wig or robe, Koome cooks at home or entertains her guests
  • At her church, she finds pleasure in teaching the Sunday school

Kenya's Chief Justice, Martha Koome, makes the most out of her social life when not honouring her busy schedule as the president of the Judiciary.

In a brief chat with the BBC, Koome said that away from her busy work, she spends time with her family and also her church, where she mentors children.

At home, she cooks for the family as well as entertaining guests.

"I am just a mum. I cook, I entertain guests, I go to the village, I go to church, I teach Sunday school," she said.

Koome made history as the first woman to have ever served in the Kenyan CJ post.

Koome at the helm of the Judiciary

In May this year, she came to the helm of the government's legal arm after flooring her male counterparts in the vetting exercise undertaken by the Judicial Service Commission (JSC).

Born and raised in Meru county, Koome went to Mugoiri Girls High School, where she emerged as the top student and secured a place at the University of Nairobi to study law.

Upon graduation in 1985, she proceeded to the Kenya School of Law for a postgraduate diploma in law which she earned in 1987, before being admitted to the roll of advocates in the same year.

During the interviews, the lady justice told the panel that she earned a master’s degree in Public International Law in 2010 from the University of London.

“I grew up in rural areas in Meru in a village so I am a villager to the truest sense. My parents were peasant farmers and we were 18 children from two mothers," she stated.

Legal journey

She began her legal profession after joining Mathenge and Muchemi Advocates, working until 1993 as an associate.

The 61-year-old advocate left Mathenge and Muchemi Advocates to run her own law firm until 2003.

She served as High Court Judge between 2003 and 2011, a time which she headed the Environment and Lands Division in Nairobi.

She was also a resident judge in Kitale and Nakuru.

100 days in office

Looking back when she succeeded David Maraga, Koome is hopeful much will be changed regarding how the Judiciary carries itself independently.

"There are feelings that we can actually do well in terms of the Judiciary. It plays the critical role that it is supposed to as the guardian of the constitution, as the guardian of our democracy, as the guardian of our rule of law, and the defender of human rights," she said.

She also insisted on her commitment to clear the backlog of cases that the judicial system grapples with and also dealing with corruption among judges.

She, however, decries the shortage of judges, which has contributed to the heaping of the cases.

The CJ also stated the preparations of the arm of government concerning the upcoming vote next year.

"We anticipate the problems that might come up. But what we would like to assure Kenyans is that we in the Judiciary are independent. We are impartial and we have no preferred candidates. We are there to uphold the will of the people," she said.
