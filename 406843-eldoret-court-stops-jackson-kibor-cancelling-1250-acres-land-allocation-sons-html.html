The Eldoret Court of Appeal has stopped prominent Uasin Gishu businessman and farmer Jackson Kibor from cancelling 1250 acres of land allocation to his sons.

The court granted a stay of execution orders based on an application by the sons dated February 17.

The appeal application was applied by Kibor sons namely Elkanah Kipleting, Evans Kipkosgei, and Erick Kipchumba after they expressed dissatisfaction with the May 6, 2020, ruling at the Environment and Land Court that ruled Kibor was the legal owner of the land.

The trio sought temporary orders stopping Kibor from implementing the judgment by the High Court until the appeal is complete.

The ruling had further instructed Land Registrar in Uasin Gishu county to nullify and cancel the consolidation, subdivision and issuance of title numbers Soy/Kapsang Block 10 (Samitoi) 13, 14, and 16 (the suit properties) to Kibor’s sons.

“The applicants argued that unless the order sought is granted, there is a likelihood that the respondent will evict them from the suit properties as he has developed hostility and intolerance against them, though they are the respondent’s sons. If execution of the judgment is carried out, the intended appeal shall be rendered nugatory,” Kibor’s sons told the court of appeal.

They also told the court that they have been occupying the properties for many years.

But with their strained relationship with their father, they argued that shortly after delivery of the judgment, Kibor orchestrated their arrest and incarceration at the neighboring Soy Police Station on account of alleged trespass.

“Unless the order sought is granted, there is no telling what the respondent may decide to do with the suit properties before the appeal is heard and determined. In the event that he evicts the applicants and disposes of the suit property, the appeal will be rendered nugatory,” the court said.

The court ruled that it was satisfied that the applicants had satisfied the twin principles for grant of an order of stay of execution of the trial court’s judgment as sought.

"Consequently, we allow the application dated 17th February 2021,” the court further noted.

Kibor had moved to court in 2016 to block the transfer of the parcels of land to his sons in an unresolved family dispute.

To his relief, the Eldoret Lands and Environment court in 2020 ruled in his favour in the case pitting him against his sons and former employees.

The court dismissed three applications tabled in court by Kibor’s sons and the employees seeking a temporary order of stay of execution of the judgment delivered on May 6, 2020, pending the hearing and determination of the intended appeal.

The judgment delivered by Justice Antony Ombwayo ruled that Kibor was the legal owner of the disputed land before the sons filed the appeal to contest the ruling.

The employees, in their application, told the court that they are senior citizens who will suffer irreparable damages if they are evicted from the disputed land.

The sons filed the notice of appeal on the judgment arguing that Kibor was likely to dispose of the suit land, hence subjecting them to substantial loss unless the application was allowed.

But Justice Stephen Kibunja dismissed the applications stating that there was no evidence showing the extent of damage that the applicants would suffer

Kibor had also claimed that his employees were illegally registered as proprietors of the parcels of land.

The court found that Kibor was the legal owner of the land and that his sons and the employees did not have valid documents to claim the land.

The judge ruled that Kibor presented in court documents that validated his ownership, unlike his sons.

“The upshot of the above is that the plaintiff has proved his case on the balance of probability and I, therefore, declare that he is the rightful owner of the original parcels of land,” the court ruled.
